Public Risk Signal Review

active

## 1. Use Case

Use this card for financial-regulation, bank takeover, risk-resolution, customer-rights, asset-quality pressure, and governance-risk topics.

It fits cases where public materials already show visible risk signals, but official disclosures have not explained the full cause. The article must balance information gain with strict claim boundaries.

## 2. Do Not Use For

Do not use this card for insider-reveal writing, responsibility hunting, shareholder-conspiracy framing, crisis clickbait, depositor-panic framing, rumor amplification, or highly emotional financial-accident narratives.

Do not use it for topics that require investment advice, price forecasts, legal conclusions, or causal conclusions not yet disclosed by official sources.

## 3. Core Expression Goal

Explain visible public risk signals in layers, while making clear which causes remain undisclosed by official sources.

The goal is not to find the "real cause" for the official side. The goal is to help readers separate:

- public evidence
- background signals
- unsupported conclusions

## 4. Expression Posture

- Tone: calm, restrained, and careful.
- Judgment strength: medium-low; make only evidence-bounded judgments.
- Evidence order: official announcements, then operating data, then penalties and governance background, then counterevidence and gaps.
- Emotion boundary: do not create panic, suspense, or "it finally exploded" hindsight drama.

Allowed boundary phrases:

- public materials show
- visible risk signals
- the available record supports
- what remains undisclosed is

## 5. Recommended Structure

1. State the officially disclosed takeover, assumption, and customer-rights arrangement.
2. Explain what risk signals were visible in public materials before the takeover.
3. Analyze operating data and asset-quality changes, including both pressure and counterevidence.
4. Treat shareholder risk, regulatory penalties, and governance signals as background rather than direct causation.
5. Separate official disclosure from undisclosed causes and missing information.
6. End with what customers and industry readers should actually watch: protection arrangement, risk recognition, disclosure boundary, and governance implications.

## 6. Opening Principle

The opening may begin with official takeover, assumption, and customer-rights facts, but it must not remain a notice summary.

It should quickly move into the article's tension: which public signals were already visible, and which causes still cannot be concluded.

Reader interest should come from evidence tension, not panic, bankruptcy language, depositor-loss language, or insider suspense.

## 7. Evidence Rules

- Official announcements: highest priority; use only for takeover, assumption, and protection arrangements.
- Operating data: use to explain asset-quality pressure and business changes; separate annual, interim, and missing comparable data.
- Regulatory penalties: use only as risk-control, credit, interbank, equity-management, or governance background; do not write them as trigger mechanisms.
- Shareholder risk: use only as governance background or risk environment; do not write it as the takeover cause.
- Media background: use only as supporting leads; never replace official, annual-report, rating, or regulatory materials.
- Comparable cases: use only for mechanism comparison; do not imply the same outcome.
- Missing data: state the gap; do not invent a deterioration chain or infer conclusions from absence.

## 8. Claims That Can Be Made

- Public materials show several risk signals had become visible before the takeover.
- The takeover, assumption arrangement, and customer-rights arrangement are supported by official disclosures.
- These signals are better treated as risk background than as direct causation.
- Some 2024 year-end indicators improved, so the risk chain must not be written as one-way deterioration.
- Public materials can show what signals appeared; they do not replace official disclosure of why the final takeover occurred.

## 9. Claims To Use Carefully

- Risk accumulation: must be tied to specific data or official materials.
- Governance pressure: must be described as background, not direct cause.
- Asset-quality pressure: must also address counterevidence and data-period differences.
- Comparable cases: must be framed as mechanism reference, not same-case inference.
- Delayed annual reports or missing data: can only be information gaps, not proof of deterioration.

## 10. Forbidden Claims

- The bank exploded.
- The bank collapsed, went bankrupt, or entered liquidation.
- Depositors have lost money or deposits are unprotected.
- Shareholder risk caused the takeover.
- Regulatory penalties directly triggered the takeover.
- Official sources have disclosed the full risk-transmission chain.
- 2025 operating conditions are known to have deteriorated.
- Zhongbang is the next Baoshang.
- It finally happened.
- What the insider truth is.

## 11. Title Direction

Titles should emphasize:

- public materials
- risk signals
- before the takeover
- undisclosed cause
- evidence boundary

Usable title types:

- public-record review
- risk-signal analysis
- takeover-mechanism explanation
- evidence-boundary framing

Forbidden title types:

- crisis clickbait
- bankruptcy headline
- depositor-panic headline
- insider-reveal headline
- shareholder-conspiracy headline
- regulatory-blame headline
- strong-causality headline

## 12. Hard Constraints For E

1. E must not add facts.
2. E must not invent a causal chain.
3. E must use boundaries confirmed by B_CONFIRM and C.
4. E must address 2024 year-end improvement as counterevidence.
5. E must state the 2025 data gap and must not invent deterioration.
6. E must frame shareholder risk as governance background, not direct cause.
7. E must frame regulatory penalties as risk-control or governance background, not trigger mechanism.
8. E must avoid unsupported collapse, bankruptcy, and depositor-loss expressions.
9. E must preserve the boundary that official sources have not disclosed the full cause.
10. E must not treat `PROCESS_PASS` as `PRODUCT_PASS`.

## 13. R3 Constraint Addendum

This card is a compliance and safety floor. It is not sufficient by itself as a complete publishability route. For publication-grade financial writing, add the R3 constraints below:

1. The title must anchor a concrete tension, such as takeover versus AA/stable rating, 2024H1 pressure versus 2024 year-end improvement, or official takeover versus undisclosed full cause.
2. The opening must start from a concrete contradiction or numeric abnormality, not from process explanation.
3. The article must include at least two logically progressive subheadings.
4. The article must move through financial-column logic: event tension, data contradiction, balance-sheet signal, regulatory action, shareholder-governance background, and customer-rights boundary.
5. Avoid generic explainer phrases such as "what this data shows," "a safer way to say it," or "what is truly worth paying attention to."

## 14. Topic Fit Note

The Zhongbang Bank takeover topic can easily drift into crisis writing, accountability hunting, insider speculation, or unsupported causality.

The upstream evidence supports takeover, assumption, customer protection, partial asset-quality pressure, and risk signals. It also contains important boundaries: 2024 year-end improvement, missing 2025 data, insufficient shareholder-risk transmission evidence, and insufficient evidence that penalties triggered the takeover.

Therefore, the topic needs a card that forces layered evidence use, counterevidence, restrained expression, and causal discipline.
